I try not to criticize any specific art medium. When I hear someone rag on
watercolor as "childish", or boldly proclaim that "rap sucks", I'm the first to
point out that they are drawing these conclusions from small, unrepresentative samples.

However, I'll now undermine that entire paragraph with the following: acrostic
poetry can absolutely get fucked. I will rail against this lazy, uninspired
drivel from Psalm 119 to that one Edgar Allan Poe piece, but today I'm here to
take aim at its most prevalent, most obnoxious use: masquerading as "helpful
mnemonics" in training courses.

Sorry, I just got back from a full day of CPR and First Aid training.

## D.R.S.A.M.P.L.E.S.A.B.C.D.

Okay, *technically* it wasn't this extreme: first they hit me with
D.R.S.A.B.C.D., to which I immediately said, out loud, "nope, seven letters is
way too damn much". For those who haven't encountered this mnemonic before, or
who rightly don't remember 100% of it for more than 30 days after hearing it:

- Danger: make sure the scene is safe
- Response: check if the patient is responding. If so, ask them what happened.
  Remember S.A.M.P.L.E.S:
  - Signs and symptoms: wait, what's happening
  - Age, athleticism, allergies: guys, what happened to D.R.S.A.B.C.D.?
  - Medication: help! I think we're stuck in *another* acrostic
  - Pertinent history: wait, what? No, you can't make the adjective the word
    in these things! You'll lose the important part: "history"
  - Last time you ate or drank anything: okay, now I KNOW you are fucking with
    me.
  - Events leading up: this couldn't have used up the above "L" for "lead-up"???
- Send for help: call 000 (or 911 for my fellow yanks) or ask a bystander to do
  it.
- Airway: open mouth, check for obstructions, tilt head back.
- Breathing: check for breathing for 10 seconds &ndash; look, listen, feel.
- CPR: start CPR.
- Defibrillation: use an AED if there's one available.

Every single situation in the course began with "first, apply the D.R.S.A.B.C.D.
response plan", painstakingly spelled out one. letter. at. a. time. I think
that's utterly useless.

## How we got here

The original acronym, when it was first introduced, was
[A.B.C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ABC_(medicine)). Over the years, there
have been many proposed changes and extensions. The C was originally there to
have you check for a pulse (circulation), but they learned that laypeople suck
at this and it's better to jump straight to CPR.

In the intervening years, apparently a lot of people have been forgetting to
first make sure the scene is safe, or they start CPR on someone who's like "hey
yo, what the fuck are you doing to me", so they extended to "DR ABC". Then they
chucked in some more so people wouldn't forget AEDs or emergency services.

What started as a crisp, memorable acronym for what you *must* do for CPR,
became a grab-bag of every conceivable thing you might need to do in any and all
situations.

## Two Types of Mnemonics

The way I see it, there are two kinds of mnemonics.

The first kind are the ones that bridge the gap while you burn something into
long-term memory. They don't have to be good (you can get away with making "P"
into "pertinent history"), they just help retrieve the information until the
mnemonic is no longer needed. This is what S.A.M.P.L.E.S. is actually good for:
EMTs, doctors, nurses, etc. These get used every day, but this is not something
a random first-aider could be expected to hear once and remember in 2 years.
Hell, I'm not embarrassed to say I couldn't remember it by the time we hit the
next slide, and I was *trying*.

The next kind is the kind that you're supposed to hear once or twice and
remember for a lifetime, and be able to pull it out in the middle of an
emergency. These only work well if they are short (3-4 MAX), have a single word
for each letter (no load-bearing adverbs/adjectives/articles/etc.), and consist
of stuff you mostly already know. F.A.S.T., for recognizing stroke symptoms, is
a great example of this:

- Face: droopy face on one side
- Arms: weakness in one arm
- Speech: difficulty speaking clearly
- Time: call an ambulance NOW; seconds matter

And it's widely regarded as effective. Unfortunately, we're seeing proposals now
for the enshittification of F.A.S.T. into monstrosities that people [don't
remember](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/JAHA.123.035696) and letters
that [don't help](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30118372/).

- [B.E.F.A.S.T.](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/STROKEAHA.116.015169)
- [N.E.W.F.A.S.T.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FAST_(stroke)#cite_note-7)
- [B.E.F.A.S.T.E.R.](https://www.themedicalcity.com/news/how-spot-stroke-be-faster)
- [G.O.T.T.A.G.O.F.A.S.T.](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z9G1Mf6TZRs)

## What I Propose

In aviation, we remind people to think "aviate, navigate, communicate" during an
emergency (even in the world of [fancy toy planes](https://carbonix.com.au/)).

- Aviate: focus on flying the damn plane. It doesn't matter where (yet).
  Prioritize the thing that can kill you in the next 30 seconds before you think
  about anything else.
- Navigate: only after you've sorted that out, your next priority is to think
  about where you are and where you are going.
- Communicate: only after you have those two things sorted out, are you to even
  think about responding to the question ATC asked you 2 minutes ago.

Notice how it's not A.N.C.? Notice how it's not A.E.T.R.A.A.A.H.B.C.G.V.H.F. for
aileron elevator throttle rudder attitude altitude airspeed heading bearing
course groundspeed very high frequency radio?

D.R.S.A.B.C.D. is mixing two very different concerns. One is general overall
guidance for every situation, and the other is what specifically to do when
someone stops responding/breathing. I propose we crack that back apart. First,
start with the guidance.

- Ensure the scene is safe
- Talk to the patient
- If needed, send for help
- Begin aid

Then, separately, you remind the students, "if the patient goes unresponsive at
any time, remember A.B.C.D." Done. That's it.

Compare these two approaches, for example, in the bee sting scenario:

- "When treating a bee sting, first apply the D.R.S.A.B.C.D. response plan"
- "When treating a bee sting, first ensure the scene is safe. Are there more
  bees nearby, perhaps a hive/wasp-nest? If so, remove the patient to a safe
  area first."

The prose version is way more memorable, and correctly emphasizes the importance
of making the scene safe; it doesn't bury it in a word that's 6/7ths dead
weight.

I know this because this is how they first taught me in my first (and only,
until today) First Aid/CPR course *18 years ago*. They repeated that line at the
top of every scenario. When we did our practice sessions, we were specifically
required by the instructor to turn our heads, look around, and then say aloud
"the scene is safe". If we didn't, we were called out for it. And **I remember
this as if it were yesterday**.

## Final Remarks

Anyway. Acrostics. Look, I'm all for constrained art forms: pixel art, haiku,
12-bar blues. You know what else is a constrained form, and also 1000% more
memorable? Limericks. Instead of D.R.S.A.B.C.D., I propose we start every
scenario in training with this:

> If you happen 'cross someone in need<br>
> Don't blindly jump into the deed<br>
> There might just be danger<br>
> If conscious, some anger<br>
> So check both before ABCD
