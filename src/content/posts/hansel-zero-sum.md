I'm a little uncomfortable with the ending of Hansel and Gretel.

Before I start, I promise this is not one of those "when you really think about
it, they were really the villains the whole time" (I'm looking at *you* Jack, of
the beanstalk fame). They were absolutely justified in killing the witch, and
who's gonna condemn them for helping themselves after?

Let's rewind first, and go over the highlights of the story real quick, in case
you're foggy on it.

- We're set in something like medieval Germany, in the height of a famine.
- Hansel and Gretel have your classic evil stepmother figure.
- Evil stepmother says "hey, husband, we're going to starve to death; go
  abandon your two children in the woods so we can live"
- So Dad fucking does it. Yeah yeah, he pushes back on the idea at first, but
  let's remember he's 100% the one who pulls the metaphorical trigger here.
- Hansel overheard the plan in the night though, so he collected a bunch of
  rocks.
- When dear old Dad takes them out into the woods (again, to abandon them and
  leave them to die), Hansel drops the rocks, one by one, behind him.
- They camp out in the woods, wake up by a dying fire, and Dad is gone.
- They follow the stones back home. Dad is overcome with relief, and learns his
  lesson.
- PSYCH! Stepmom tells Dad to try again, and he fucking tries the exact same
  thing again.
- They start locking the door at night so Hansel can't collect rocks this time;
  he decides to try using bread crumbs.
- Well that doesn't work; birds eat them.
- Dad ditches the children, they get truly lost this time.
- They wander around the woods for days, nearly starving to death.
- You remember this part: they come across a house made of sweets, they start
  eating it, a witch with poor eyesight imprisons them, sets Gretel on the task
  of fattening up her brother so the witch can eat him, they do some clever
  tricks to delay it and ultimately Gretel tricks the witch into the oven she
  intended to cook Hansel in (I absolutely love this part too; straight-up
  sketch comedy), yada yada.
- They steal a bunch of gems and treasure from her house.
- They wander around the woods for a long time again.
- They ride on the back of a duck to cross a lake for some fucking reason (don't
  worry, they did this one at a time; two kids riding on a duck would have been
  absurd)
- They find their way home.
- Stepmom is dead (I think starvation is implied, but they literally don't say),
- Dad is revealed to have been very miserable, understandably, for twice trying
  to murder his children.
- The family uses their treasure to spend the rest of their days well fed.

## A Caveat

There's no truly definitive edition of this story. This is an oral tradition
that was famously catalogued by the Brothers Grimm, and their versions evolved
over time. For this piece though, I'm going to call [the 1857
edition](https://books.google.com.au/books?id=y9tLAAAAIAAJ&pg=PA79&redir_esc=y#v=onepage&q&f=false)
"canon".

## Famine... or maybe inflation

Let's look at the opening paragraph:

<img src="/images/hansel-grimm-1857.png" class="invertible" lang="de" alt="Vor
einem großen Walde wohnte ein armer Holzhacker mit seiner Frau und seinen zwei
Kindern; das Bübchen hieß Hänsel und das Mädchen Gretel. Er hatte wenig zu
beißen und zu brechen, und einmal, als große Teuerung ins Land kam, konnte er
das tägliche Brot nicht mehr schaffen">

Taking the [translation from folklorist D. L.
Ashliman](https://sites.pitt.edu/~dash/grimm015a.html):
> Next to a great forest there lived a poor woodcutter with his wife and his two
> children. The boy's name was Hansel and the girl's name was Gretel. He had but
> little to eat, and once, when a great famine came to the land, he could no
> longer provide even their daily bread.

The key word here in the original German is *große Teuerung*, which I gotta
shoot straight here, doesn't necessarily translate to "great famine". The Grimm
brothers didn't use the word *Hungersnot*.

[Another translation I
found](https://www.grimmstories.com/language.php?grimm=015&l=de&r=en) puts it
like this:
> Near a great forest there lived a poor woodcutter and his wife, and his two
> children; the boy's name was Hansel and the girl's Grethel. They had very
> little to bite or to sup, and once, when there was great dearth in the land,
> the man could not even gain the daily bread.

So maybe it was just extreme inflation? I'm going to choose to take the famine
interpretation here, but at the end, I invite you to re-analyze the
micro-economics of the inflation interpretation and see if it changes the
outcome; I'm going to casually assert it doesn't.

## The Ending

Hansel and Gretel, and Dad of the year, live the rest of their lives in pure,
unbridled joy, and all their problems are over. Once again, I'll invoke the
original German:

> Da hatten alle Sorgen ein Ende, und sie lebten in lauter Freude zusammen.

[grimmstories.com](https://www.grimmstories.com/language.php?grimm=015&l=de&r=en):

> Then was all care at an end, and they lived in great joy together.

[Ashliman](https://sites.pitt.edu/~dash/grimm015a.html):

> Now all their cares were at an end, and they lived happily together.

Now, on its own, I'd take the "lauter Freude zusammen" bit to be the joy of
being together again as a family, but with the "Sorgen ein Ende", it strongly
suggests to me more than just family cohesion. This was, in my reading, not only
an end to their hunger, but living in surplus.

And this is my problem. This is a famine. Gretel rocking up with an apron full
of jewels doesn't create more food in their community: there simply isn't enough
grain in their town to feed everyone. Every loaf of bread they buy is a loaf of
bread that someone else will not be able to have. The jewels merely give them
the power to outbid their neighbors and jump the queue. But I think it's
probably worse.

I'd wager the difference between the "brink of starvation" and "lauter Freude"
[is at least a factor of
two](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minnesota_Starvation_Experiment). It wasn't
enough that someone else is starving in their place, no, *they went back for
seconds*.

In the end, they became the very villain they destroyed: happy, rich, and eating
other children (by proxy).
